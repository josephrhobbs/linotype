I've been thinking a lot about contact and complementarity constraints. How do we build a solver that can natively handle implicit contact without convergence issues?

Purpose

In trajectory optimization, contact can either be modeled implicity or explicitly. Explicit models for contact require contact mode scheduling... the solver must know which modes of contact occur and in what order they must occur. This is rather difficult when you move to highly contact-rich systems. How could you reasonably schedule contact in a centipede? It'd be a disaster!

Implicit contact sounds much better, but solvers struggle significantly with implicit contact. Implicit contact is handled by adding the bilinear constraint \( f_i d_i = 0 \), where \( f_i \) is contact force and \( d_i \) is signed distance to contact. It is also important to constrain \( f_i, d_i \ge 0 \). This enforces no penetration, no attraction, and no action at a distance.

The constraints \( f_i, d_i \ge 0 \) are simple to enforce because they are linear inequalities. The bilinear equality \( f_i d_i = 0 \) is nonconvex and difficult to implement in practice. As \( f_i \rightarrow 0 \) or as \( d_i \rightarrow 0 \), there's a "sharp corner" in the feasible set, making optimization very difficult.

Methods

This attempt at control through contact uses semidefinite relaxations of nonconvex programs. For each time step, we define the matrix \( X_i \) according to

\[ X_i := \begin{bmatrix} 1 & f_i & d_i \\ f_i & f_i^2 & f_i d_i \\ d_i & f_i d_i & d_i^2 \end{bmatrix} . \]
