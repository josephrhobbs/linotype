There are more people in the world today than there ever have been in human history.

Take a moment to contemplate that thought. It's staggering, isn't it? And at the same time, it's painfully obvious; global population has been growing since the dawn of humanity, and this trend will continue at least until the end of this century... although the United Nations has postulated that this trend will reverse by the end of this century due to declining birth rates.

Globally, more than half of people live in urban areas, and as the century progresses, we can expect this proportion to increase from about 55% to 68% by mid-century. Some of these urban areas are more pleasant (and practical) to live in than others; crime, limited transportation, atmospheric and water pollution, and other factors can cause urban areas to be both undesirable and unhealthy. Furthermore, anthropogenic climate change caused by unsustainable lifestyles is causing unprecedented environmental destruction as cities grow. This raises what is perhaps the most important globally relevant question for humankind at this point in our development: how do we build urban areas that sustainable for people, sustainable for the natural environment, and support a pleasant and practical daily life?

I am not an urban planner, and I do not claim to know the right answers to this question. But I've offered it to invite you into the right headspace for thinking through the question I do discuss in this essay.

Academic publishing is accelerating at an unprecedented rate as the global research community at large grows. In the calendar year preceding this writing, over 3 million academic publications reached print. Much like the global population, there are more academic publications now than there ever have been in human history. And as academic publication accelerates, so must associated artifacts, like conference posters, textbooks, course notes, lecture videos, industry publications, ... the list goes on. It is only natural, therefore, to ask what is perhaps the most important meta-research question: what does it mean to build sustainable and practical research ecosystems? Or, to borrow from the urban planning analogy: what are the right principles for planning, building, and maintaining research cities: ecosystems for publication, education and pedagogy, and commercialization?
